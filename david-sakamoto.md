```text
Netflix raised prices of its monthly subscription. The internet is outraged. Wall Street is not. Here's why this matters for B2B subscription businesses.

For your convenience, I built the chart below to help us track Netflix price changes over the years. What it reveals is not a story about price hikes, but rather a story about pricing architecture.

A few things stand out:

1. The Premium trendline is steeper than the Basic line.

In 2013, Premium launched at $11.99 — just $4 above Basic at $7.99. Today, Premium is $26.99, and the entry-level ad tier is $8.99. The gap went from $4 to $18.

That's not inflation. That's deliberate tier separation. Netflix systematically widened the spread between its highest and lowest plans to capture the full spectrum of willingness to pay.

2. The ad tier is a churn absorber, not just a cheap plan.

Every time Netflix raises the price on Standard or Premium, the ad tier catches customers who would otherwise cancel. This is brilliant: The floor protects the ceiling.

Does churn spike after a price increase? Yes, briefly. After the January 2025 hike, churn rose from 1.8% to 2.5%. By May, it was back to 2.0%. And half of those who cancel return within 6 months.

(Please read Dan Rayburn's, industry expert, detailed comments on this.)

Think of it, Netflix doesn't avoid churn. It absorbs it. Nearly 1 in 2 new sign-ups now choose the ad tier. That's not a discount plan. That's a retention engine.

3. Small increases, high frequency.

Netflix doesn't do big pricing shocks. It does $1–$2 increases every 12–18 months. Each one feels like nothing.

But do the math: ~80 million U.S. subscribers, a $1–$2 raise per tier. That's over $1 billion in annualized incremental revenue. From a price increase, most people shrug off (for now).

The gap between hikes has compressed from ~24 months to ~14 months. Netflix is training its customers to expect regular price increases, just as the best recurring-revenue businesses should normalize frequent price adjustments.

But here is the part most companies get wrong:

Netflix invests in value BEFORE it raises price. Live sports. WWE. MLB. Podcasts. Games. They earn the increase, then ask for it.

Most B2B companies do the opposite. They leave pricing untouched for three years, then surprise customers with a 20% jump and wonder why renewal rates drop.

Happy customers will pay more — if you've given them a reason and you ask at the right time.

If you're adding AI to your product, that's your moment. Deliver the value first. Then adjust the price.

Netflix doesn't have a pricing strategy. It has a pricing system that adheres to scientific principles. That's the difference.
```
